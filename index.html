Portfolio Case Study

Price Check

Turning locked SaaS pricing data into a self-service product and a compounding data moat

Hannah Garner · Staff Product Manager

01
Context

Vendr was transforming from
service company to platform

  • Before Staff augmentation model — procurement negotiation specialists handling deals for enterprise customers. Manual. High-touch. Expensive.
  • Shift Post-Blissfully acquisition — platform still fragmented. Enterprise market tapped out. Needed to move downstream & scale without adding headcount.
  • Asset $3B+ in SaaS transaction data locked in manual processes — Power BI dashboards, Airtable, hand-delivered PDFs. Took days per request.

Vendr was the trusted source for SaaS pricing. The brand promise: we tell you whether your price is fair — definitively. The data was the moat. But it was trapped behind a service team.

02
Product Principles

The strategic framework
that guided every decision

◆ Customers don’t come to us

We go to them — buyer guides, Chrome extension, embeddable APIs.

◆ Big rocks, not gravel

Focus on the top 100–200 suppliers where data is deep and demand is highest.

◆ Data fuels price transparency

Every contract uploaded enriches the AI. The product gets smarter with every user.

◆ Educate, not just solve

Show the bell curve, the data points, the reasoning. Build trust through transparency.

03
Problem Definition

SaaS pricing is completely opaque

There are no public benchmarks for what software actually costs. Buyers Google it, find nothing, and sign contracts blind.

Vendr had the answer — $3B+ in real transaction data — but it was locked behind a service motion. Our analysts used the data to negotiate on behalf of customers. Powerful, but it didn’t scale.

Market

No one publishes real SaaS transaction prices. Every purchase — new or renewal — starts with “Contact us for pricing.”

Demand

Massive organic traffic to our pricing pages — people were searching for this data.

Gap

$3B+ in transaction data, but only accessible through a services team negotiating on your behalf. The insight existed — the self-serve product didn’t.

04
Who we’re building for

“Help me understand if I’m
getting a good price.”

The SaaS buyer Sales manager renewing Salesforce. Director of finance reviewing 20 contracts this quarter. Marketing lead re-signing HubSpot.

“I got a renewal quote and I just want to know — is this a fair price? That’s it.”

No leverage, no data

They can’t push back on a vendor without evidence. There’s no “Kelley Blue Book” for software. They’re signing blind.

Not data savvy — and don’t want to be

They don’t want a spreadsheet or a bell curve. They want someone they trust to say “you’re overpaying” or “this is fair.”

Vendr was that trust

Real transaction data from thousands of deals. The only source that could say definitively what software actually costs. Our brand was the answer.

05
Strategy & Goals

Build the data moat —
make the product smarter with every use

LLMs alone can’t produce trustworthy pricing intelligence — you need well-labeled, curated datasets. No existing service could help us build them. So the bet was: use Price Check to collect the data, structure it into a proprietary engine, and expose it through APIs.

< 1 min Upload to pricing insight
20,000+ Products with pricing data
100% free Self-serve, no sales team required

Price Check wasn’t just a product — it was the data collection engine for building a structured pricing catalog that no competitor could replicate.

07
Building the Product

Starting small, learning fast

Handcrafted PDFs · delivered in-app · 48–72 hr turnaround

I set the discovery strategy — sitting in on live procurement calls, running weekly sessions with our SaaS buying specialists, and interviewing customers mid-renewal to understand what they actually needed:

What did people want in the data?

Pricing benchmarks, not product reviews. Fair price, not feature comparison.

What was important?

Speed, specificity, and trust in the numbers. Buyers needed confidence before a negotiation.

What were we learning?

Every handcrafted report taught us what to extract, what to score, and what to automate.

The Triad team — product, design, engineering — met regularly to synthesize these learnings and shape the product direction.

A deliberate concierge experiment: ~20 handcrafted reports a day, each one teaching us what to extract, what to score, and what customers actually valued — before we automated a single thing.

Measuring Outcomes

Closing the loop

Every Price Check ended with a rating. Below 3 stars triggered a personal email from the team — we wanted to understand what fell short, not just count completions.

≤ 3 stars → structured diagnosis

What specifically missed? Data accuracy, range too wide, irrelevant comps — each option mapped to a product fix.

4+ stars → open-ended prompt

“What would make this more useful?” — surfaced expansion ideas directly from satisfied users.

Average rating: 4.5 stars. The feedback loop didn’t just measure satisfaction — it told us exactly what to build next and confirmed when we’d earned trust.

12
Challenges

Staying disciplined when
the appetite was everywhere

▲
CEO-led scope pressure “Can we add reviews? Can we compete with G2?” — Lots of appetite to go in many directions. Used Mixpanel data to show that users only engaged with pricing and negotiation data. Reviews got almost zero interaction.
▲
Defining what we are not Price Check answers “am I getting a fair price?” — not “what product should I buy?” Held that line against pressure to become a product comparison tool.
▲
The “free” cultural shift A service company giving away its core insight required trust in the data flywheel. Built the business case around data enrichment — every upload deepens the moat and feeds the pricing catalog and APIs.

14
Reflection

What I would do differently

  • De-scope the supplier universe earlier. We tried to provide some form of value for every contract uploaded, even when data was thin. The degraded experience (negotiation insights only) may have diluted perceived product quality. I’d have been more ruthless about which suppliers we launched with.
  • Understand the full buyer lifecycle sooner. We had a strong SEO strategy that attracted people to pricing data, but getting someone to upload their contract at the right moment was far harder than driving awareness. The timing problem — reaching buyers when they actually have a document in hand — was the real conversion challenge.
  • Appreciate contract complexity earlier. We did a good job supporting POs, quotes, PDFs, renewals, and annuals — but understanding which data are present at which stage of the buyer journey was more complex than I initially realized. Being clearer upfront about what we’d accomplish and when would have set better expectations and sharper phasing.
