03
Problem Definition

SaaS pricing is completely opaque

There are no public benchmarks for what software actually costs. Buyers Google it, find nothing, and sign contracts blind.

Vendr had the answer — $3B+ in real transaction data — but it was locked behind a service motion. Our analysts used the data to negotiate on behalf of customers. Powerful, but it didn’t scale.

Market

No one publishes real SaaS transaction prices. Every purchase — new or renewal — starts with “Contact us for pricing.”

Demand

Massive organic traffic to our pricing pages — people were searching for this data.

Gap

$3B+ in transaction data, but only accessible through a services team negotiating on your behalf. The insight existed — the self-serve product didn’t.

Vendr had $3B+ in real transaction data — but it was locked inside a service motion. The bet: give it away for free through a self-serve tool. Every buyer who uploads a contract gets an instant answer. Every upload makes the next answer better.

< 1 min Upload to pricing insight

05
Who we’re building for

“Help me understand if I’m
getting a good price.”

The department head IT director evaluating a new CRM. Head of finance comparing contract terms across 20 vendors. Marketing lead buying HubSpot for the first time. They buy software for their teams — it’s not their full-time job.

“We’re about to sign a new contract and I have no idea if this price is fair.”

They research before they talk to anyone

80%+ of buyers look up pricing before ever talking to sales. 50%+ walk away entirely if they can’t find it. They don’t want a deck — they want an answer.

Opaque pricing erodes trust

Hidden pricing encourages negotiation games, creates artificial urgency, and lengthens sales cycles by 30%+. Buyers become frustrated. Sellers pay in higher CAC.

Vendr had the data to fix it

$3B+ in real transaction data across 20,000 products. The only source that could tell a buyer definitively: “you’re overpaying” or “this is a fair price.”

Building the Product

The foundation was already there

For six months, our team had been building an internal document processing tool with Extend.ai — extracting structured pricing data from the contracts and quotes flowing through Vendr every day.

Tens of millions of pages — contracts, quotes, order forms, renewal notices

Extraction at scale

Extend.ai processed 10+ document types at 95%+ accuracy — from 30-page legal agreements to complex order forms with nested tables.

Schema & normalization

SKU mapping, pricing tiers, and scoring models turned raw contract data into structured, comparable intelligence.

Locked behind services

Analysts used this tool internally to prep data for negotiations. Powerful, but only accessible through our services team.

I led the Extend.ai integration — iterating on extraction prompts, tuning models against edge cases, and defining the accuracy thresholds. This became the intelligence backbone for everything we built next.

08
Discovery

Learning the domain from the inside

The data lived in our back office

Handcrafted PDFs · delivered in-app · 48–72 hr turnaround

I set the discovery strategy — sitting in on live procurement calls, running weekly sessions with our SaaS buying specialists, and interviewing customers to understand what they actually needed:

What did people want?

Pricing benchmarks, not product reviews. Fair price, not feature comparison.

What was important?

Speed, specificity, and trust in the numbers. Buyers needed confidence before a negotiation.

What were we learning?

Every handcrafted report taught us what to extract, what to score, and what to automate.

A deliberate concierge experiment: ~20 handcrafted reports a day, each one teaching us what to extract, what to score, and what customers actually valued — before we automated a single thing.

Challenges

Data coverage gaps

14
Reflection

What I would do differently

  • Be honest about what the data can and can’t tell you. We got Extend.ai to 95%+ accuracy across 10+ document types — but accuracy varied by vendor, contract type, and deal stage. Setting clearer thresholds for when a Price Check was “ready” vs. “directional” would have improved the degradation tiers and set better buyer expectations from the start.
  • Lower the first step, sooner. Our strategy was “every upload makes the product smarter” — but uploading a contract is a high-friction ask. Buyer Guide was already driving strong SEO traffic to pricing pages. I would have invested earlier in a low-friction price estimate — even without a document — to widen the funnel. The tradeoff: those users don’t feed the data flywheel. That tension needed to be confronted in strategy, not discovered in conversion metrics.
  • Launch fewer suppliers, better. The principle was “big rocks, not gravel” — top 200 suppliers where data was deepest. But we still returned something for every upload, even when data was thin. The negotiation-insights-only experience drove below-3-star ratings and diluted trust. I’d have launched a tighter set and expanded only as coverage earned it.
